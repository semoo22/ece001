The birth of robots:

"Badi' al-Zaman Abu al-Izz bin Ismail bin al-Razzaz al-Jazari," a Muslim scholar, was born in 1136 AD on an island that was called "Abu Omar", from which his nickname "Al-Jazari" came. This island was part of the Levant, and today it belongs to Turkey and is located directly on the border line with Syria. What is curious? In this person he was the first man-made animatronic or "robot". At the request of the emir of "Dyarbakir", called "Amed", to make a machine that would replace the servants whenever he wanted to perform ablution for prayer. Al-Jazari made a machine in the form of a boy, erect, with a jug of water in one hand and a towel in the other. In addition to the presence of a bird on the boy’s turban, which whistles whenever the time for prayer comes, and then this boy or servant comes to his master “Amid” to pour water for him in a certain amount, and when he finishes ablution, he presents him with a towel and returns to his place. This "boy" or "prayer robot" was questioned For many scientists, how to know prayer times, in our current world and with technological development, electronic cells can be relied on to act on pre-programmed directions by humans, providing directions and steps that must be taken by the robot. Scientists put a lot Among the hypotheses that discuss how the robot knows prayer times, and how it moves. One of the most prominent of these theories is its dependence on sunlight that enters the room in which it is located, so that a little water evaporates enough to move the joints of the robot, and to urge it to move forward or backward depending on the situation. On the control of several precise scales placed inside this body. This theory applies only during the day and not at night. Al-Jazari's most notable inventions included self-propelled water-powered instruments, measuring instruments, musical instruments, water clocks with a self-alarm system, and the elephant clock In which he collected several parts and tools that represented the civilizations and cultures of his time, and now it is called the "United Nations Hour". Al-Jazari was considered one of the pillars of the Islamic scientific renaissance, which formed the basis for Western scientific civilization later. His book, "The Combined Knowledge" The useful work in crafting tricks” is one of his most prominent books, which he spent about 25 years writing. This book was the main reference for mechanical and hydraulic machines in the Middle Ages, and what distinguished it was the presence of drawings and designs for these machines, and it has been translated into many languages. Most of his manuscripts are in Turkey, such as the Topkapi Museum in Istanbul, and the other part of his manuscripts and studies were bought by several Western countries, including the Netherlands, France (the Louvre Museum), the United States of America (Boston Museum and Oxford University Library), Japan, and Britain.
